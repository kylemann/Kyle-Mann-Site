Case Study: Dupont Circle Hotel

Website Usability Testing

The doyle collection is an Irish family owned Luxury Boutique Hotel company that has 8 properties in the US, Ireland and the UK. When I started studying UX and Design, I was working concurrently as the Doyle Collection's Dupont Circle Hotel on the marketing, sales and event team. The company re-designed and launched a new website, and I served on the web committee, which gathered feedback from management and guests about the site, and proposed changes to the corporate office. The launching of a new website presented a great opportunity to get my feet wet with learning about UX research by conducting a usability study. The guest experience doesn't start at check in, it begins when they land on your site; I proposed my study to management and received their support.

To begin my study, I interviewed to the different department heads and learned about what their feedback and challenges were with the website. It was an ideal start by learning about their perspective, as the operations team has a lot of face to face time with the guest and had the best insight into the guest expectations, as defined by what they see on the website.

Testing Overview

The usability test was formatted to learn about the user’s interaction, browsing and navigation through the website. The data collected was primarily qualitative findings, through observations of the user behavior from visual presentations and assigned tasks.

The actual testing took place at participants home and/or offices, which was prefered as it was a natural environment (one participant was tested remotely), and each session lasted f between 60-90 minutes. The Users were asked to search and find the “Dupont Circle Hotel” main landing page, and describe their initial reaction and were free to browse the site, while expressing their perceptions. The users were then assigned specific tasks and/or scenarios to navigate through, while the I observed their behavior4. The tasks, scenarios, and observations were formulated based on the following objectives to gain user insight

4observing participant behavior to find:

  • Ease of use
  • User understanding of the material
  • Learning ability - how easy it would be for most users to learn to navigate the website
  • Look and feel appeal – homepage’s content
  • User perception of site content – Including visuals and writing
  • User navigation through site information and structure

Results, Findings and Reccomendations

Generally, the key findings5 showed all participants had several issues throughout their navigation of the site and tasks6; mostly based on content information confusion, lack of clarity while navigating and lack of clickability. The general participant perception of the property was that the property was luxury, hip, stylish and European owned.

5

  • Confusion determining information about the “Doyle Collection”
  • Confusion about the room types offered
  • Confusion about what hotel amenities are offered/included
  • Confusion about what “Level 9” offers and/or what it is generally
  • Confusion about the website writing content and language used (British slang)
  • Irritation from the “Book Now” box on all pages
  • Irritation with the booking calendar’s slow speed and European-style date entry
  • Extreme Irritation at booking higher rates than displayed in the “rooms” table, where the rooms offer a rate “starting at”
  • Lack of manual date inquiry entry, in the booking calendar
  • Lack of accessible GHA/Doyle Collection general information
  • Lack of information about dining options (namely “Bar Dupont” not listed)
  • Lack of accessible room amenity details
  • Lack of name/description of displays/visuals

Findings Solutions
Users (5/6) were confused about European dates being presented in the booking tool – Date/Month/Year Use American date format (Month/Date/Year) (Updated)
Users (3/6) were irritated by the slow navigation of the calend Put a drop down menu for the “month/year” selection for quick month selection without scrolling(*) (Updated)
Users (3/6) were confused when they could not enter the dates manually, (*) Implement manual desired date entry into the date box in booking tool
Users were confused about package add-on features (4/6 unsure about what the package included)(*) - This prompted the users to want to learn more about packages (3/6). In addition, some users were interested in ensuring the room preferences were addressed prior to hitting the “Book Now” button (2/6). Under the “See more Rates and Packages”, located in the booking rate display page – replace to “See more Rates, Packages and Preferences”. Then once the user clicks the drop box and selects preferred room option, there are then two tabs displayed– “Recommended Add-Ons” tab (offering package rates and clickable details) and the other tab “Preferences” (Offering room preferences, including high/low flow, handicap, special notes etc.)
Users (3/6) were confused about room options presented after entering an inquiry in the booking tool – Non-Refundable (listed twice) vs. Best Available Rate. Users expressed irritation about the rate being offered at a lower rate for “Non-Refundable”, yet having “Best Available Rate” option offered at a higher rate. The top of the room type rate option should read “Rates Starting at XXX” and delete the “Book Now” button. The user will then be more focused on the rate options/package rates available below, and will understand the distinction between the different rate options.
Through the user navigation of the booking process, some users accidently left the page (5/6) – once they decided to return to the booking process, and were disappointed the dates had to be re-entered and weren’t saved. Once a user enters desired dates, the dates should be saved in the booking tool during the user's browsing session, even if they leave the booking process

The most promising sketch, it seemed, was a single page layout with a fixed navigation to the left. I liked this design because it offered the user a convenient navigation through the information, while allowing the content to be in a 100% viewport height container for easier reading. I drafted the mock-up design in HTML/CSS to see how it looked in the browser but the results were disappointing5, though there were some aspects I liked6. In addition, the fonts the client requested7 were not indicative of the studio’s desired academic, yet fun image. Since I had spent so much time designing this version, I kept trying to salvage what I had to make it better, but it just didn’t work. This experience proved to be one of the most valuable design lessons – Don’t be afraid throw away a bad design and start over, from the beginning.

Before going back to the drawing boards, I connected with the client to discuss the challenges with the requested fonts. Sharing my research about Typography, I walked him through my thought process to finding the right type. Influencing my selctions, was based on the studio’s primary teaching of Haydn’s work and other prolific classical music from the 1700’s – mid 1800’s. I expressed the importance of the right typeface choice to properly represent the personality of his studio, and emphasized the extensive research and trial and error that goes into creating outstanding typographic styling. The client understood and offered me full creative control in this realm, another valuable lesson, address issues sooner, not later, don’t wait!. With that, I went back to the drawing board10 with a new perspective and a fresh start.

The ideal layout that evolved separated the sections into individual pages, allowing the content to be more digestible and provided more space for the images to pop out. Designed to ensure the landing page11 1) included the logo of the studio, 2) what it does tagline, 3) navigation options, and 4) a way to learn more. The opening paragraph statement is intended to offer a warm welcome to any prospective students, while the complementary imagery reinforces the studio’s ambiance and services. For the mobile version, I included an auto scrolling parallaxing image gallery12 to the landing page and removed the scattered images to prioritize the written content, yet still offer the user some imagery of the studio without taking up valuable mobile real estate. Also, I included a map13 to the mobile homepage and contact to provide easy access to students navigating to the studio via their mobile devices.

Toward the later part of the development process, I wanted to get a temperature check on the overall design of the site. Reaching out to fellow designers and friends, I received some key critical feedback and findings: 1) A user reported having initial trouble going back to the home page, because there was not a link in the navigation, but eventually clicked the logo to get back. This was an oversight on my part, though the logo was linked to the homepage, all pages needed to be included in the navigation - therefore I added the “about” to link back to the landing page. 2) Another user reported that while they liked the site welcoming design of the pages, he felt it was missing the same personal connection from Matthew, the teacher. This influenced the final design of the contact page24, including a final statement in Matthew’s words thanking the user for visiting and inviting them to get together over coffee to discuss their musical journey.

Deploy and Conclude

Overall, this site was a significant lesson in empathy. Feeling and understanding what others are expressing, even if you don’t agree with them, is a powerful way of connecting. Whether I was seeking to understand what users were looking for, or proposing an alternative solution to the client - An empathetic approach not only sharpened my ideas, but got to the bottom of what’s relevant and important.

lessons learned

  • Don’t ever be afraid to throw away bad designs and start over.
  • Always seek the user’s feedback from the beginning, middle and end - listen and implement
  • Continually ask why, and be certain to find out why.
  • When pushing back to the client, have well thought out alternative proposals and solutions ready to present.
  • Relax, and enjoy, it’s a process.
