Case Study: Studio V

Web Design | Development | Photography

Matthew Viator is the owner and teacher of Studio V, a Washington DC based musicology and piano studio. I met Matthew a few years ago when I began studying music with him, later leaving to pursue my design studies. I approached him to design a new website, an ideal opportunity to grow my design skills, as his studio had evolved immensely over the years and needed a new web presence indicative of that change.

View Studio V Website

developed and deployed using: HTML, CSS, Javascript/Jquery, Brackets text editor, Photoshop, Amazon S3, Amazon Route 53, Git Hub, and lot's of Classical Music.

Discover and Define

We started the project with a discussion over breakfast about the studio’s services, objectives, and needs1. My focus was to get a feel for how the studio evolved over the years, what kind of brand image was desired, the ideal student, and understand the why behind the answers. This was purely an informative interview, I left my worldview at the door.

1client needs

  • Site to feel academic, yet modern and fun.
  • Include fine print terms, conditions, and studio policies
  • Pricing for the various student levels, without using the terms “beginner, intermediate, and advanced”
  • Attract students who are serious and committed
  • Include a Gotham and Gainsborough font in the design

After learning the client needs, I began to identify the prospective user needs2. Studio V hosts a monthly social, comprised of currently enrolled students, which I attended and used as an opportunity to conduct informal research. It’s a very friendly and dedicated group of musicians - they were thrilled to give me feedback on the upcoming site design. Based on my discussions and questions to fellow students, I discovered the most important website needs for them were: location, pricing, and styles taught. I inquired about their thoughts on the Studio’s previous website3, which did not include: the needs listed, a logo, location, or studio images. Students noted that the previous site did include studio policies and expectations, which they found helpful, but that they found the formatting confusing.

2user needs

  • Location
  • Pricing
  • Styles Taught
  • Review

3clients previous site

I returned to the client to discuss my findings, reservations, and confirm agreement on the edited written content. First, I proposed that he consider revising the pricing terminology to be titled “beginner, intermediate, and advanced” vs. “proficient, technical, and professional.” I was concerned for two reasons: 1) That the user would be confused about what pricing was appropriate for them and 2) that the terminology would affect SEO negatively, as a common user search would be “beginner piano lessons Washington DC”. The client understood it was confusing, and shared concern of the SEO risk; he agreed to include “beginner, intermediate, and advanced”, as descriptive terms only. Secondly, I formatted and revised the written content for the Studio’s welcome, personal bio, lesson description, and studio policies. The Studio policies were lengthy and unorganized, so my edits reformatted them and separated them to be in two sections: lesson and pricing policies. This allowed the fine print to be more digestible and relevant to the featured topics, and was helpful later in organizing the content in design. The client approved the proposed edits, and the written content was ready. Next, I set up a couple photo shoots during studio hours in order to gather as much site collateral as possible prior to diving into design sketches.

Design and Develop

My design strategy began with reviewing the content to identify themes, sections, and navigation architecture from the written content. One of the key findings that influenced the final design layout is the reoccurrence of groups of three: 3 social media review links needed, 3 price structure, 3 contact details. Once I established a content structure, I started drafting layouts, sketching4 one idea after another, literally every idea I had.

4brainstorm - sketches

The most promising sketch, it seemed, was a single page layout with a fixed navigation to the left. I liked this design because it offered the user a convenient navigation through the information, while allowing the content to be in a 100% viewport height container for easier reading. I drafted the mock-up design in HTML/CSS to see how it looked in the browser but the results were disappointing5, though there were some aspects I liked6. In addition, the fonts the client requested7 were not indicative of the studio’s desired academic, yet fun image. Since I had spent so much time designing this version, I kept trying to salvage what I had to make it better, but it just didn’t work. This experience proved to be one of the most valuable design lessons – Don’t be afraid throw away a bad design and start over, from the beginning.

5dissapointing results

  • Logo was way too large
  • Navigation to the left was an eye sore
  • One page layout did not work, given the breadth and quantity of content (reviews, policies, pricing etc.)
  • Requested font selections didn’t reflect the studio’s academic branding desire

6results i liked

  • Palette of grey, white, and burnt orange themed color
  • The navigation of lessons, tuition, review, and contact sections – and in that order

9client typeface request | gainsbourgh and gotham

Before going back to the drawing boards, I connected with the client to discuss the challenges with the fonts he requested. I walked him through my thought process to finding the right type, which for this project was influenced by type styling popular in the 1700’s – mid 1800’s, because the Studio focuses on work from this time, most notably primarily teaching Haydn’s work. Showing him the fonts requested in use, I emphasized the extensive research and trial and error that goes into creating outstanding typographic styling. The client agreed the requested fonts were not working and offered me full creative control in this realm. Another valuable lesson, address issues sooner, not later, don’t wait!. With that, I went back to the drawing boards with a new perspective and a fresh start.

The ideal layout that evolved separated the sections into individual pages, allowing the content to be more digestible and provided more space for the images to pop out. Designed to ensure the landing page10 1) included the logo of the studio, 2) intro tagline, 3) navigation options, and 4) a way to learn more. The opening paragraph statement is intended to offer a warm welcome to any prospective students, while the complementary imagery reinforces the studio’s ambiance and services. For the mobile version, I included an auto scrolling image gallery11 to the landing page and removed the scattered images to prioritize the written content, yet still offer the user some imagery of the studio without taking up valuable mobile real estate. In addition the mobile version includes a map12 to provide easy access to students navigating to the studio via their mobile devices.

The lessons page opens with an encouraging welcome, including the benefits of classical music and another teacher’s favorite quote to exude the Studio’s personality. The opening drop cap13 styling adds to the academic feeling desired, in addition to drawing the reader's attention. After the intro, the Studio’s styles and methods are summed up with the section titled “What Styles Will I Learn?”, which answers a common prospective student question. Organized from earlier content edits, the lesson policies are sectioned into digestible chunks and styled with small-caps 14 which allows the user to scan through quickly to review and find what is pertinent to them.

Due to the pricing page’s unusual title terms (Proficient, Technical, Professional), I was seeking a pricing option design15 that would clearly identify to the user what title was relevant to their skillset. Ultimately, the best solution was to ask and then answer the question: “Who’s this for? This solution is space efficient, and very quickly communicates the relevant pricing.

15pricing layout

When considering the social media review links location I didn't want them to potentially bounce users out of the site, by only adding a link to the nav-bar or footer. The solution was to add a dedicated review page to the site16 , that displayed the links in the same theme of 3’s format and personalized reviews from current students of different skill backgrounds17 , by design to connect and attract any prospective student’s reviewing with a similar skillset.

Toward the latter part of the development process, I wanted to get a temperature check on the overall design of the site. Reaching out to fellow designers and friends, I received some key critical feedback and findings: 1) report of having initial trouble going back to the home page while browsing, because there was not a link in the navigation, but eventually clicked the logo to get back. This was an oversight on my part, though the logo was linked to the homepage, all pages needed to be included in the navigation - therefore I added the “about” to link back to the landing page. 2) Another user reported that while they liked the site’s welcoming design of the pages, he felt it was missing the same personal connection from Matthew the teacher. This influenced the final design of the contact page19, including a final statement in Matthew’s words thanking the user for visiting and inviting them to get together over coffee to discuss their musical journey.

Deploy and Conclude

Overall, this site was a significant lesson in empathy. Feeling and understanding what others are expressing, even if you don’t agree with them, is a powerful way of connecting. Whether I was seeking to understand what users were looking for, or proposing an alternative solution to the client - An empathetic approach not only sharpened my ideas, but got to the bottom of what’s relevant and important.

lessons learned

  • Don’t ever be afraid to throw away bad designs and start over.
  • Always seek the user’s feedback from the beginning, middle and end - listen and implement
  • Continually ask why, and be certain to find out why .
  • When pushing back to the client, have well thought out alternative proposals and solutions ready to present.
  • Relax, and enjoy, it’s a process.
